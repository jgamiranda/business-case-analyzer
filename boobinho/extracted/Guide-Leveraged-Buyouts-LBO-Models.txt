Overview & Key Rules of Thumb 
Questions about leveraged buyouts and leveraged buyout (LBO) models are the most advanced  
ones you’ll receive in entry -level investment banking interviews.  
These question s are less important  than the ones on Accounting, Equity Value  and Enterprise 
Value, and Valuation/DCF Analysis for several reasons:  
1) The topic is more advanced , so most questions about it will be fairly simple . 
 
2) LBO models are not relevant for all finance roles  – For example, you won’t receive 
many LBO questions in equity research  or asset management interviews.  
 
3) This topic is more important for private equity (PE) interviews , where you’re 
guaranteed to get questions and case studies involving  leveraged buyouts.  
The first few sections of this guide are  the most important ones . 
Even if you’re interviewing for a role such as equity research, you still need to be familiar with 
the concept  of leveraged buyouts .

Key Rule #1: What is an LBO, and Why Does It Work?  
Private equity firms  raise capital from outside investors, called “Limited Partners” (pensions, 
endowments, insurers, funds of funds, etc.) and use it to buy companies, improve them, and 
sell them to realize a return on their investments . 
They use leverage to buy companies because leverage – borrowing funds from someone else – 
amplifies their returns . 
In other words, if a deal produces a 10% annualized return, with leverage, it might produce a 
15% or 20% annualized return.  
But it also works the other way: If the PE firm loses money  in a deal, it will lose even more 
money if it uses leverage. This is why they are always concerned with the downside risk . 
Another benefit of leverage is that it allows PE firms to invest in more companies  because they 
contribute less of their capital to each one – but this is a secondary benefit.  
After raising capital from outside investors, the private equity business model works like this:  
1) PE firms search for companies that might be undervalued  or misunderstood and that 
could produce high returns if managed properly . 
 
2) Then, just like a real estate investor might buy a house using a down payment and a 
mortgage, the PE firm uses Cash  (called “Investor Equity”) and Debt  to buy a company.  
 
3) The private equity firm run s the company  for several years and makes “improvements,” 
like the renovations that a real estate investor might make.  
 
4) Eventually, the PE firm sell s the company  and uses the proceeds to repay the remaining 
Debt it borrowed to buy the company. If all goes well, it will earn back a multiple of its 
Investor Equity and earn a high internal rate of return (IRR).  
Many sources compare a leveraged buyout to buying a house, but it’s more like buying a house 
to rent out to others and eventually sell  rather than buying a house to live in.

Yes, we earn less cash flow in Years 1 – 5 because we must pay interest and repay Debt when

of our money in the deal. 
This is why it’s critical to consider a range of scenarios  in LBO models.  
The Legal Structure That Makes LBOs Possible  
When a PE firm acquires a company, it does not “own” the company directly.  
Instead, the PE firm typically forms a “holding company,” which it owns, and this “holding 
company” acquires the real company . 
The banks and other lenders that provide the Debt in the deal then lend to this Holding 
Company so that the Debt stays at the “HoldCo” level.  
The managers and executives that retain ownership after the deal takes place also own shares 
in this Holding Company:

This structure is important because it means that the private equity firm is NOT “on the 
hook” for the Debt it uses in the deal! It’s up to the Target Company to repay it.  
The PE firm not only borrows other people ’s money  to do the deal, but it doesn’t even borrow 
the money directly: The company  borrows money so the PE firm can do the deal!  
And if something goes horrifically wrong, it’s the company’s fault.  
Private equity firms do assume reputational  risk from this arrangement; a firm can’t go around 
buying companies, loading them up with Debt, and then abandoning them.  
Not only would the firm’s returns plummet, but companies would also stop agreeing to do 
deals with the firm.  
However, this legal structure still gives PE firms huge advantages and greatly reduces the firm -
wide risk . This structure is one of the reasons why politicians frequently attack private equity 
(and they have a point – if a firm’s investments do poorly , there should be consequences).  
Ideal Leveraged Buyout Candidates  
There are 100 million+ companies globally, but only a small percentage are ideal leveraged 
buyout candidates . 
First, most companies are too small for leveraged buyouts – a family business with $1 million in 
annual revenue and 10 employees is great for the family, but it’s too small for PE firms.  
Some boutique and middle -market PE firms execute smaller leveraged buyouts, but even these 
“smaller deals” are usually worth at least $5 – 10 million.  
Assuming the size is right, the price must also be right. 
Almost any deal could work at the right price , and even the best company could be a terrible 
LBO candidate if the price is too high.  
High prices create significant risk because the higher the price, the higher the probability of that 
price falling over time; few companies trade at high multiples forever.  
After size and price, an ideal LBO candidate must also be able to service its Debt, so stable cash 
flows  are extremely important.  
A pre -revenue biotech or tech startup is the worst  possible leveraged buyout candidate 
because it’s extremely risky and does not yet have positive cash flows.  
Besides these points, PE firms usually look for the following financial qualities in LBO 
candidates:

• Revenue / Expenses:  Low fixed costs, high recurring revenue, and high profit margins.  
• Balance Sheet:  Significant  fixed assets (e.g., factories, buildings, and equipment) that 
can act as collateral when issuing Debt.  
• Cash Flow Statement:  Stable cash flows with minimal required Capital Expenditures 
(spending to build and maintain long -term assets like factories and equipment).  
• Valuation:  Low to modest valuation multiples compared with the peer companies.  
Stability  is much more important than growth potential  in standard leveraged buyouts.  
If a company can support a huge Debt load, repay some of it, and grow modestly, that’s enough 
to make many leveraged buyouts work.  
The qualitative factors  that PE firms seek include:  
• Management Team:  Experienced team that has worked together for a long time and 
that is participating in the LBO by rolling over some of their equity.  
• Industry/Market:  Frag mented market with moderate growth potential, competitive 
advantages for the company, and relatively light regulation.  
For example, an industry where the #1 company has a 5% market share, with companies #2 – 
50 all having market shares between 1% and 5%, is more appealing than one where the top 3 
companies own 80% of the market.  
This is because many PE firms pursue add-on acquisitions  to make their portfolio companies 
bigger and more valuable, and it’s easier to acquire smaller companies in fragmented markets.  
Finally, private equity  firms must consider their exit strategies and sources of returns  in deals. 
They almost always prefer the following:  
• Markets with feasible M&A exits  (i.e., they can sell the company to a normal company 
or another private equity firm rather than taking it public).  
 
• A potential IRR or annualized return in the 20 – 25% range , with a low chance of losing 
money in the downside cases.  
 
• Deals with a mix of returns sources , i.e., the company’s core business grows, becomes 
slightly more valuable, and  repays significant Debt over the holding period. PE firms do 
not want to bet on deals that require a company’s valuation to double for the math to 
work.

Key Rule #2: The Purchase Price and Sources & Uses Schedule  
As with merger models, you can make LBO models extremely complex , but complexity won’t 
necessarily produce more useful  information  or better investment results.  
You can build a simple LBO model in 3 steps:  
1) Set up the transaction assumptions , including the Purchase Price and Sources & Uses 
schedule  (this section of the guide focuses on this step ). 
 
2) Project the company’s cash flows  and Debt repayment  (covered in Key Rule #3 ). 
 
3) Make the exit assumptions , calculate the IRR and money -on-money multiple, and assess 
the returns drivers ( covered in Key Rule #4 ). 
For private companies , the Purchase Price is almost always based on a multiple of EBITDA.  
The Debt used to fund the deal is based on the Leverage Ratio (Debt / EBITDA) used in similar, 
recent deals in this market.  
You may also make assumptions about the company’s M inimum Cash balance, tax rate, and the 
fees associated with the deal .

If you’ve read the M&A guide, it’s almost the same idea here; the main difference is that Stock  
is not available as a Source of Funds in leveraged buyout s. 
As with acquisitions of private companies, most leveraged buyouts of private companies are 
done on a cash -free, debt -free basis , which means the target company’s existing Cash and Debt 
both go to $ 0 when the deal closes … and then get replaced immediately.  
The company’s Cash balance is used to repay as much Debt as possible, and any remaining Cash 
after that is distributed to the selling shareholders.  
If there’s not enough Cash to repay the Debt – such as $50 of Cash and $80 of Debt – then the 
$50 of Cash is used to repay $50 of Debt, and the proceeds to the selling shareholders are 
reduced by the remaining $30. This $30 deduction is then used to repay the remaining Debt.  
It’s like how homeowners must repay their mortgages with the sale proceeds when they sell 
their homes.  
The new Debt used to fund the deal replaces the company’s previous Debt, and the company’s 
Minimum Cash replaces its previous Cash balance; the Investor Equity acts as the “plug.”  
Investor  Equity is like the down payment when buying a house.

Key Rule # 4: Make the Exit Assumptions and Calculate the Returns  
For a private equity firm to realize an acceptable IRR, it must exit  its investment in the 
company.  
The three  main exit strategies in leveraged buyouts are:  
1) M&A  – Sell the company to another company or a different private equity firm within 
the next 3 – 7 years.  
 
2) IPO – Take the company public and sell the stake gradually over time.  
 
3) Ongoing Dividend Recap AKA “Hotel California”  – The PE firm never “sells” the 
company but instead has the company issue Dividends continually. Eventually , if these 
Dividends get big enough, quickly enough, the firm might realize acceptable returns.  
PE firms overwhelmingly prefer M&A exits  because they are clean and simple and result in 
immediate proceeds.  
They also tend to produce the highest IRRs because firms do not have to wait for years to sell or 
recover everything.  
In some cases, a PE firm may be unable to sell a portfolio company in an M&A exit, such as if 
the company is too big to be acquired, there are no good buyers, or it’s in an emerging market 
without much deal activity.  
These “alternate exit” scenarios involving IPOs and Recapitalizations are unimportant for case 
study and modeling test purposes. Focus on the standard M&A exit.  
The mechanics  of the exit calculations in LBO models are simple: Assign an “exit multiple” to 
the company’s EBITDA, multiply to get the Exit Enterprise Value, and then subtract the Debt 
and add the Cash (subtract “Net Debt”) to get the Exit Equity Proceeds.  
These proceeds go to the PE firm, and you can calculate their annualized return based on these 
Exit Equity Proceeds and the Investor Equity in the beginning.  
In our simple LBO model, we used the most common exit assumption : An acquisition at an exit 
multiple equal to the purchase multiple . 
That’s a reasonable baseline assumption , but you might change it based on the company’s 
growth rates and Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) :

Calculating both the MoM multiple and the IRR is useful because it tells us how a deal looks

For example, if the MoM multiple looks acceptable but the IRR does not, we might be able to

Returns Attribution  Analysis  
We can also create a “Returns Attribution Analysis” to determine the deal  drivers : 
 
The formula s are as follows:

• Returns from EBITDA Growth:  (Year 5 EBITDA – Year 0 EBITDA) * EBITDA Purchase 
Multiple  
 
• Returns from Multiple Expansion:  (EBITDA Exit Multiple – EBITDA Purchase Multiple) * 
Year 5 EBITDA  
 
• Retu rns from Debt Paydown/Cash Generation:  Total Return – Returns from EBITDA 
Growth – Returns from Multiple Expansion  (or Initial Debt – Final Debt + Final Cash – 
Initial Cash – Transaction and Financing Fees ). 
With the EBITDA Growth calculation, you’re saying, “Let’s imagine that the Purchase Multiple 
stayed the same  – how much value would we get solely  from the company’s EBITDA growing?”  
The Multiple Expansion calculation  uses similar logic: “Let’s imagine that the company’s initial 
EBITDA were the same as its final year EBITDA – how much value w ould we get solely  from the 
multiple increasing?”  
In a real model, you would also create sensitivity tables  to look at different outcomes . For 
example, what happens when the Purchase Multiple, Exit Multiple, Debt Used, and Revenue 
Growth Rates and EBITDA Margins change?  
Most private equity firms target specific ranges  for the IRR and MoM multiple, and these tables 
help you assess the outcomes in different scenarios.  
For example, a middle -market private equity firm might plan to hold companies for an average 
of 5 years  and aim for the following financial results : 
• Downside Case:  Minimum of a 1.5x MoM multiple, with no specific minimum IRR . 
 
• Base Case:  A 2.0x MoM multiple and a 15% IRR . 
 
• Upside Case:  A 3.0x MoM multiple and a 25% IRR . 
Private equity funds are j udged on their IRRs , as their own investors (“Limited Partners”) 
allocate their money based on the average annualized returns.  
If a PE firm consistently achieves 20%+ IRRs over 5 or 10 -year time frames, it will be able to 
raise capital more easily.  
But if it returns its LPs’ money too quickly or its IRRs are low or inconsistent, it will have 
difficulty raising additional capital.

IRR and MoM multiple change under different conditions.  
For example, if we find that the MoM multiple drops to 1.5x even in the most pessimistic case, 
we might recommend doing the deal.  
But if it drops to 0.5x, we might reject  the deal because we’d lose  money  in that case, which is 
too much of a risk for most PE firms.

Key Rule #5: Quick IRR Math for Interview Questions  
Interviewers may describe a leveraged buyout and then  ask you to approximate the IRR.  
They might also give you the IRR and ask you to estimate the Exit Multiple , Purchase Price, Debt 
Used, or EBITDA.  
These questions are far more common in private equity and growth equity  interviews, but 
they may still come up in investment banking interviews . 
Therefore, it’s good to know the basics , even if you cannot answer the most difficult questions.  
You can approximate IRR if you know the Money- on-Money Multiple  and the holding period. 
The most important rules are shown below:  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
21 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
• 2x Multiple in 3 Years : ~25% IRR  
• 2x Multiple in 5 Years : ~15% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 3 Years : ~45% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 5 Years : ~25% IRR  
There are other ways to approximate the IRR as well; for example, you could use the “Rule of 
72” to determine the years required to 2x your money at different IRRs (e.g., 72 / 8 = 9, so it 
takes 9 years to 2x your money at an 8% IRR).  
There’s also the “Rule of 115” for 3x multiples.  
Another trick you can use to “simulate” the effect of compounding is the following:  
• IRR for a 2x Multiple : 100% / # Years * ~75%  
• IRR for a 3x Multiple : 200% / # Years * ~65%  
• IRR for a 4x Multiple : 300% / # Years * ~55%  
For example,  if a PE firm invests $100, receives  and contributes nothing during the holding 
period , and earns $300 from the exit in Year 6 : 
• IRR for a 3x Multiple:  200% / 6 * ~65% = ~2 2% (mental math: 200% / 6 is the same as 
100% / 3, which is 33% ; 2/3 of 33% is 22% because 1/3 of 33% is 11% )  
If you run the math in Excel, the IRR is 20%, which is close enough to be useful.  
These rules do not work as well once the holding periods get much longer or the deal 
scenarios become more unusual, so you cannot use them in every situation.  
However, in 99% of interview scenarios, the basic approximations work quite well:  
• 2x Multiple in 3 Years:  ~25% IRR  
• 2x Multiple in 5 Years:  ~15% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 3 Years:  ~45% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 5 Years:  ~25% IRR  
If you get a period such as 4 years to exit, you can average the IRRs.  
And if there are Dividends or an IPO Exit, you can estimate the “average year” of the exit and 
use that and the MoM multiple in the calculations.

ANSWER:  Start with the multiple and period:  
• Multiple: $100  / $50 = 2x.  
 
• Period:  The “average exit year” in this IPO is Year 4.  
 
• IRR: A 2x multiple in 3 years is a 25% IRR, and a 2x multiple in 5 years is a 15% IRR. So, a 
2x multiple in 4 years should be around a 20% IRR.  
In Excel, the IRR is ~ 19%, so we’re within 1%.

QUESTION #4: A PE firm acquires a $100 EBITDA business for a 10x multiple, and it believes it 
can sell it again in 5 years for the same 10x multiple.

• Investor Equity:  $100 * 10 * 40% = $400.  We know it’s 40% Equity because the total

• Multiple / IRR:  A 25% IRR over 5 years is a 3x multiple.

• Net Debt Upon Exit:  There’s $600 of Debt in the beginning and $300 left at the end.

To calculate the Cash Flow Surplus / (Shortfall), we start with the Beginning Cash, add the Free 
Cash Flow, subtract the mandatory Debt repayments (“Amortization”), and subtract the 
Minimum Cash, which is ¥20 billion here.  
The formula for the Revolver (Repayments) / Drawdowns is : 
= – MIN(Cash Flow Surplus, Beginning Revolver)  
If there’s a Cash Flow Surplus, we use it to repay the Revolver balance . 
If there’s a Cash Flow Shortfall, we draw on the Revolver to borrow extra. 
We can never repay more than the Revolver itself because even if the Cash Flow Surplus is 
bigger  than the Revolver , the formula takes  the minimum between these two.

But this deal also includes a management options pool and an equity grant to the Mezzanine

The instructions in this case study are very specific about the treatment: The Options Pool 
increases  the diluted share count, and if the options are exercised in the exit, they increase the

Meanwhile, the Mezzanine Equity grant does not  create new shares; it’s a simple percentage 
granted to the Mezzanine investors after the exercise of the options and the distribution of 
their proceeds.  
We assume the options are exercisable if the Exit Equity Value exceeds the initial Investor 
Equity from the Sources & Uses schedule . This is a trick to estimate whether the exit share price 
is higher than the exercise price without  tracking the share count s over time.

Options Pool %) to exercise them.  
The (1 – Options Pool %) in the denominator grosses up the share count so that if there are 100 
basic shares, a 10% options pool results in 111.11 total shares, with the options holders owning 
11.11 shares , or 10% of this total.  
If the options are exercised, the company’s Equity Value increases because its Cash goes up, 
and the shareholders  are responsible . 
Therefore, the Equity to Management Options is based on Options Pool % * (Exit Equity Value +

Part 8: Case Answers, Investment Recommendation, and LBO Valuation  
In this deal, Bain Capital was targeting a 30% IRR  over a 4 -5-year holding period. Since we easily 
achieve that 30% IRR here, the investment recommendation is an easy “yes.”  
The full rationale is the following:  
• Even if the exit multiple  falls from 7.5x to 5.5x, in -line with the purchase multiple, the 
sponsor IRR is still ~30%, and even if the final EBITDA margin is slightly lower (6.5% 
rather than 7.5%), the IRR remains above 30%.  
 
• Approximately 60% of the total equity returns  come from EBITDA G rowth and D ebt 
Paydown and C ash Generation, with only 40% from M ultiple Expansion, and the 
company can easily service its Debt past the first few years.  
 
• The biggest risk factor  is that the Long -Term Care Revenue is driven by pricing increases 
rather than higher patient counts . But even if the average annual pricing increases by 
only 1% per year, the sponsor IRR is approximately 37%. And e ven if the average patient 
count and pricing stay the same over 5 years, the IRR is still 34 -35%.  
That’s all you need for investment recommendations in short case studies like this one: “Y es” or

Performance Incentives: Waterfall s, Rollovers, Options, and Earn -Outs  
A PE firm might want to offer performance incentives  to the management team after a 
leveraged buyout.  
They can do this by allowing the management team to roll over their shares , offering an options 
pool , or by offering earn -out payments  (all covered in previous sections).  
Another incentive scheme  is the waterfall returns structure , which is easiest to illustrate with a 
simple example.  
Let’s say the management team owns 5% of the comp any after the leveraged buyout. 
Normally, the PE firm would receive 95% of the exit proceeds , and the m anagement team 
would receive 5%. 
But if the PE firm wants to incentivize the management team, it could structure the returns like 
this instead:  
• Up to 10% IRR:  Management gets 5%, and the PE firm gets 95%.  
• Between 10% and 15% IRR:  Management gets 10%, and the PE firm gets 90%.  
• Between  15% and 20% IRR:  Management gets 15%, and the PE firm gets 85%.  
• Above 20% IRR:  Management team gets 20%, and the PE firm gets 80%.  
“Between 10% and 15% IRR” means the cash flows that correspond to that specific IRR range . 
If a deal uses $1,000 of initial Investor Equity and generates $3,000 in Exit Equity Proceeds:  
• 10% IRR:  Corresponds to $1,611 in proceeds.  
• 15% IRR:  $2,011 in proceeds.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
68 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
• 20% IRR:  $2,488 in proceeds.  
So, initially, we distribute 95% of the first $1,611 in proceeds to the PE firm and 5% to the 
management team.  
Then, in Tier 2, we distribute 10% of ($2,011 – $1,611) to management and the remaining 90% 
to the PE firm.  
In Tier 3, we distribute 15% of ($2,488 – $2,011) to management and 85% to the PE firm.  
Finally, we distribute 20% of ($3,000 – $2,488) to management and 20% to the PE firm.  
We then add up all these distributions to calculate the totals and IRRs for each group.  
The waterfall returns structure reduces the PE firm’s IRR by ~1 – 2% while increasing the 
management team’s IRR by ~15 – 20% . 
Private equity firms might offer this structure because a ~1% IRR reduction is immaterial, but 
offering management the ability to nearly  double their IRR  is a huge incentive.

Preferred Stock and Shareholder Loans  
In addition to the standard tranches of Debt, such as the Revolver, Term Loans, Senior Notes, 
Subordinated Notes, and Mezzanine, Preferred Stock  is another way to finance deals.  
Despite the name, it is more like Debt  than Equity.  Its key features include:  
• Interest:  Preferred Stock has “Preferred Dividends” rather than interest, and these 
Preferred Dividends are not tax-deductible. The Preferred Dividend Rate also tends to 
be higher than normal interest rates, so Preferred Stock is the most expensive financing 
method.  
 
• Cash vs.  PIK: The Preferred Dividends may be paid in cash, but they could also be 
accrued or “paid in kind” (PIK), which means they accrue to the principal and increase it 
in each period. A mix of Cash and PIK Dividends is also possible.  
 
• Seniority / Repayment Priority:  Preferred Stock is junior to all other forms of Debt, so it 
has the lowest repayment priority in a bankruptcy or liquidation.

Why would a company choose  to use Preferred Stock, given that it’s more expensive than all 
the other financing methods except for Common Stock?  
It wouldn’t – but sometimes PE firms want  to use Preferred Stock to “lock in” their returns and 
guarantee a certain IRR on a portion of their investment.  
For example, let’s say the Preferred Stock Coupon is 12%, and the PE firm invests using 1 / 3

If the Common Equity IRR in the deal is 25%, the Preferred Stock “drags down” the returns 
slightly because the PE firm earns 12% on 1 / 3 of its investment and 25% on the other 2 / 3.  
But if the Common Equity IRR is 0% ( i.e., something goes horribly wrong), Preferred Stock can 
turn the deal from “breakeven” to “not great, but at least it earned a bit of money.”  
A variation of Preferred Stock is the Shareholder Loan , which is more common in Europe than 
North America.  
It works almost the same way as Preferred Stock, but with two key differences:  
1. Tax-Deductible Interest  – Unlike Preferred Dividends, the Shareholder Loan Interest 
(almost always accrued or PIK) is  tax-deductible.  
 
2. Equity Classification  – In many deals , the Shareholder Loan is counted as a simple 
component of Equity in the exit calculations, so there’s no “separate payment” made to 
the Shareholder Loan investors.  
Like Preferred Stock, the Shareholder Loan reduces the downside risk for the PE firm, but it’s 
cheaper because of the interest tax deduction.  
Also, if it is classified as a part of Equity, the Shareholder Loan might not  dampen the returns 
when the deal performs well (but  this varies by region and deal).

Original Issue Discount (OID)  
When an LBO takes place, the Debt may be issued  at a discount to its Face Value.  
This happens  most often when a bond’s coupon rate  is below the yields on similar bonds, and 
the company needs to incentivize investors with a discount.  
For example, if the bond’s Face Value is $1,000, maybe investors can buy it for $950 instead.  
The investors still earn $1,000 * Bond Coupon Rate in interest and receive back the $1,000 
upon maturity, even though they pay $950. This lower upfront price boosts the bond’s yield . 
For example, if the bond matures in 5 years and has a 10% coupon rate, normally, the investors 
earn a 10.0% yield.  
But if they pay only $950 upfront, they earn an 11.4% yield because the $50 discount adds 
around 0.2 – 0.3% per year to the annualized returns.  
This $50 difference is called the “Original Issue Discount” (OID).  
Modeling OID in all scenarios is complicated, but the basics are straightforward.  
In an LBO model, OID appears on the Uses side of the Sources & Uses schedule, along with the 
Financing Fees:  
 

 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
79 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
You then amortize the OID over the Debt’s maturity, deducting the amortization on the Income 
Statement and adding it back as a non -cash expense on the Cash Flow Statement.  
Each year, the Book Value of the Debt on the Balance Sheet increases due to this OID amortization, but the Face Value stays the same. Therefore, the Interest Expense on Debt also stays unchanged (assuming no principal repayments, PIK interest, etc.).  
It works the same way as the Amortization of Financing Fees in this simple scenario, which is why Financing Fees and OID are usually grouped in the Sources & Uses schedule.

1. What is a leveraged buyout, and why does it work?  
In a leveraged buyout (LBO), a private equity firm acquires a company using a combination of 
Debt and Equity , operates it for several years, and then sells the company at the end of the 
period to realize a return on its investment.  
During the ownership period, the PE firm uses the company’s cash flows to pay for the interest 
expense on the Debt and to repay the Debt principal.  
LBOs work  because leverage amplifies returns : If the deal performs well, the PE firm will realize 
higher returns than if it had bought the company with 100% Equity . 
But leverage also presents risk s because it means the returns will be even worse if the deal 
does not perform well.

3. Walk me through a basic LBO model  (without the full financial statements).  
“In an LBO model, in Step 1, you make assumptions for the Purchase Price, Debt and Equity, 
Interest Rate on Debt, and other variables such as the company’s revenue growth and margins.  
In Step 2, you create a Sources & Uses schedule to show how much Investor Equity the PE firm 
contributes  and how items like the transaction fees and the company’s Cash balance affect this 
contribution.  
In Step 3, you project the company’s Income Statement and its partial Cash Flow Statement 
down to Free Cash Flow.  
Then, in Step 4, you use the Free Cash Flow, Beginning Cash, and Minimum Cash to determine 
how much Debt principal the company repays each year. You then link the Interest Expense on 
this changing Debt balance to the Income Statement so that FCF deducts the Interest.  
Finally, in Step 5, you make the exit calculations, usually assuming an EBITDA Exit Multiple, and 
you calculate the IRR and Money -on-Money multiple based on the proceeds the PE firm earns 
at the end  vs. its Investor Equity in the beginning .” 
 
4. Can you explain the legal structure of a leveraged buyout and how it benefits the private

company” acquires the real company.  
The banks and other lenders lend to this Holding Company so that the Debt remains at the 
“HoldCo” level.  
Managers and executives that retain ownership after the deal closes  also have  shares in this 
Holding Company.  
This structure is important because it means that the private equity firm is NOT  “on the hook” 
for the Debt it uses in the deal: it’s up to the acquired company to repay it.

5. What assumptions impact a leveraged buyout the most?  
The Purchase Price and Exit assumptions, usually based on EBITDA multiples, make the biggest 
impact on a leveraged buyout.  
A lower Purchase Multiple results in higher  returns , and a higher  Exit Multiple results in higher  
returns.  
After those, the amount of Debt used, based on either a percentage of the Purchase Price or a 
multiple of EBITDA, makes the biggest impact.  
If the deal performs well, more leverage will make the numbers even better , and vice versa if it 
does not perform well.  
Next, the company’s revenue growth, EBITDA margins, and cash flow profile also make an 
impact because they influence the exit calculations and the Debt repaid in the holding period.  
Finally, the interest rates, principal repayments, and other terms of the Debt may make a 
difference as well, but they tend to be less significant than everything above.

8. How do you use an LBO model  to value a company, and why does it set the "floor

IRR Calculations  
Questions about quick IRR calculations in leveraged buyouts are common in private equity 
interviews, but they could also come up in investment banking interviews.  
The key is to remember the Rules of Thumb  covered earlier in the guide : 
• 2x Multiple in 3 Years  = ~25% IRR  
• 2x Multiple in 5 Years  = ~15% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 3 Years  = ~45% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 5 Years  = ~25% IRR  
If you get a scenario like a Dividend Recap or an IPO Exit , think about the Average # of Years  it 
takes for the PE firm to receive all the Equity P roceeds.  
For example, if the company goes public in Year 4 , and the PE firm sells 1/3 of its stake in Year 
4, 1/3 in Year 5, and 1/3 in Year 6, the “average exit year” is Year 5.  
 
1. How do you calculate the internal rate of return (IRR) in an LBO model, and what does it 
mean?  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
86 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
The IRR in an LBO is “the effective compounded interest rate ” or the “average annualized 
returns.” For example,  if you invest $100 in the beginning and get back $200 after 5 years, what 
compound interest rate would turn that $100 into $200 by the end?  
You calculate the IRR by making the Investor Equity (C ash) that a PE firm contributes a negative, 
and then using positives for Dividends to the PE firm and the Net Proceeds to the PE firm upon 
exit.  
Then, you apply the IRR function in Excel to all the numbers, ensuring  that you’ve entered “0” 
for any periods where there’s no cash received or spent.  
If there are no Dividends or other distributions or contributions in between purchase and exit : 
IRR = (Exit Proceeds / Investor Equity)  ^ (1 / # Years) – 1 
 
2. How can you quickly approximate  the IRR in an LBO? Are there any rules of thumb?  
The key rules include:  
• 2x Multiple in 3 Years  = ~25% IRR  
• 2x Multiple in 5 Years  = ~15% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 3 Years  = ~45% IRR  
• 3x Multiple in 5 Years  = ~25% IRR  
If you get a multiple or holding period between these, you can use averages to estimate the IRR 
(e.g., a 2.5x multiple in 5 years is a ~20% IRR).

The Purchase Price, Debt, and Sources & Uses Schedule  
The questions in this category are all more advanced  than the ones in the previous sections, 
which dealt with the basic concepts behind an LBO and how to calculate IRR quickly . 
These questions  are more likely if you’ve had significant work experience or you’re interviewing 
for private equity roles . 
 
1. What’s the true purchase price in a leveraged buyout  of a public company , and why do you 
create a Sources & Uses schedule?  
The “true price” in any deal is the Purchase Enterprise Value, but the starting point for a public 
company is typically the Equity Purchase Price : the cost of acquiring all the common shares.  
The Sources & Uses schedule clarifies this by explaining the treatment of Cash, Debt, Fees, and 
other items that will affect the PE firm’s required equity contribution.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
92 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
For example, the treatment of the company’s Excess Cash (distributed to all shareholders vs. 
partially retained) may affect Investor Equity, which is why it appears on the Sources side.  
Existing Debt rarely affects the Investor Equity because it’s almost always replaced with new 
Debt in the same amount, so it’s effectively an entry on both sides of the schedule.  
The New Debt used to fund the deal also appears on the Sources side.  
From the PE firm’s perspective, the Investor Equity line that acts as the “plug” in the S&U 
schedule is the true price they are paying (but you could  also argue that the Purchase Equity 
Value or Purchase Enterprise Value are the true prices).  
 
2. How does the Sources & Uses schedule differ in a cash -free, debt -free leveraged buyout of 
a private company?  
The Uses side starts with the Purchase Enterprise Value rather than the Purchase Equity Value 
because the company’s Debt and Cash go to 0 immediately after the deal closes. Fees also 
appear on the Uses side, as does the Minimum Cash.  
The Sources side is similar, as it includes both New Debt and Investor Equity, but in a cash- free, 
debt -free deal, it does not  include Excess Cash because the Excess Cash has already been 
accounted for on the Uses side (Purchase TEV subtracts Total Cash, and the Minimum Cash is a 
positive, so effectively the Uses side subtracts Excess Cash, which is equivalent to showing 
Excess Cash as a positive on the Sources side).  
 
3. OK, but what if this private company has additional Working Capital targets/requirements?  
When there’s a “Working Capital target,” the Purchase Enterprise Value is adjusted up or down 
based on the company’s Working Capital at the deal close, and the PE firm contributes 
additional WC funding or “takes” excess WC.  
The net effect is that the PE firm always pays the same amount (Adjusted Purchase Enterprise 
Value + /- WC Funding ), but the selling shareholders receive different amounts based on the 
Working Capital level at the close.  
For example, if the Purchase Enterprise Value is $1000, the Working Capital target is $100 upon 
deal close, and the company has $50 in Working Capital, the Purchase Enterprise Value is 
adjusted down to $950 on the Uses side, and there’s a positive entry on the Uses side for $50 in 
Working Capital funding.

93 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
The PE firm still pays $1000 total, but it’s split up into the $950 purchase price and $50 in WC 
funding, and the selling shareholders receive $50 less in proceeds.  
 
4. Is there any real difference between cash -free, debt -free leveraged buyouts and non -cash -
free, debt -free ones? 
They are economically equivalent in most cases because the company’s existing Debt is almost 
always refinanced and replaced, and the Cash is normally distributed to the shareholders as the 
deal closes  (and replaced with a new Minimum Cash balance).  
Immediately after the deal closes, the Cash balance on the Balance Sheet should equal the 
company’s Minimum Cash.  
A non -CF/DF deal would be different only if some Excess Cash remained on the Balance Sheet  
rather than being distributed. 
 
5. How do  you determine how much Debt a PE firm might use in an LBO and how many 
tranches there would be?  
You look at recent, similar LBOs and use the median Debt / EBITDA levels from them as 
references; you could also look at highly leveraged public companies in the industry and check 
their Debt / EBITDA levels . 
For example, if the median Debt / EBITDA for LBOs has been 5x, with 2x Term Loans and 3x 
Subordinated Notes, you might use those same figures.  
Then, you would test these  assumptions  by projecting the company’s leverage (Debt / EBITDA) 
and coverage (EBITDA / Interest) ratios over time . 
If they hold up reasonably well – e.g., the company’s coverage ratio always stays above 2x – 
then you might stick with the original numbers . If not, you have to try different assumptions.  
 
6. Can you describe the different types of Debt a PE firm might use in a leveraged buyout and 
why it might use them?  
Broadly speaking, Debt is split  into Secured Debt  and Unsecured Debt , which some people also 
label “Bank Debt” and “High- Yield Debt” or “Senior Debt” and “Junior Debt.”  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
94 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Secured Debt consists of Term Loans and Revolvers, is backed by collateral, tends to have 
lower, floating interest rates, may have amortization  (required principal repayments) , and uses 
maintenance covenants such as limits on the company’s Debt / EBITDA  and EBITDA / Interest.  
Early repayment of principal is allowed, maturity periods tend to be shorter (~5 years up to 10 
years), and the investors tend to be more conservative.  
Unsecured Debt consists of Senior Notes, Subordinated Notes, and Mezzanine, and is not 
backed by collateral; interest rates tend to be higher and fixed rather than floating, there is no 
amortization, and it uses incurrence covenants (e.g., t he company can’t sell Assets above a 
certain dollar amount).  
Early repayment is not allowed, maturity  periods tend to be longer (8 -10+ years), and the 
investors tend to be hedge funds, merchant banks, and mezzanine funds.  
 
7. Why do the less risky , lower -yielding forms of Debt amortize? Shouldn’t amortization be a 
feature of riskier Debt to reduce the risk?  
Amortization reduces the credit risk but also reduces the potential returns. Since risk and 
potential returns are correlated, amortization should be a feature of less  risky Debt.  
For example, if $100 million of 10% coupon rate bonds stay outstanding for 10  years, and the 
company fully repays them after 10 years, the investors earn a 10% IRR on those bonds.  
But if there’s amortization or optional repayment, that balance decline s to less than $100 
million by the end, so the investors earn less than  a 10% IRR . But the investors also assume less 
risk because they earn back their capital earlier in the period.  
The other issue with early repayment is that it credits interest -rate risk for the lenders: they’ll 
have to redeploy their capital elsewhere, but what if interest rates have fallen?

it has the choice between two capital structures with similar leve rage levels ? 
Term Loans are less exp ensive than Subordinated Notes since interest rates are lower, and they 
give the company more flexibility with its cash flow s since optional repayments are allowed in 
most cases.

planning to divest assets , make acqu isitions, or spend a huge amount on CapEx, any of which 
might be forbidden by the incurrence covenants found in Subordinated Notes.  
 
9. Why might a PE firm do the opposite and use Subordinated Notes instead?  
On the surface, this doesn’t make much sense because Subordinated Notes are more expensive 
than Term Loans . 
However, a PE firm might prefer Subordinated Notes if they doubt a company’s ability to 
comply with the maintenance covenants found in Term Loans (e.g., if it s EBITDA is projected to 
decline for a few years).  
Also, if the company wants to avoid paying cash interest  (or the PE firm has doubts about its 
ability to do so) , it may opt for Subordinated Notes with Paid -in-Kind (PIK) Interest so that the 
interest accrues to the loan principal.  
 
10. What’s the point of assuming a Minimum Cash balance in an LBO?  
The point is that all companies need some minimum amount of C ash to run their businesses, 
pay employees, and produce and deliver products  and services.  
So, if you assume that all  the company’s Cash is distributed to shareholders or used to repay 
Debt when the deal takes place, fine, but it needs to be replaced with some minimum amount 
as the deal closes so the company can keep operating.  
This Minimum Cash balance appears on the Uses side of the Sources & Uses schedule in a cash-
free, debt -free deal, and it affects the Excess Cash on the Sources side of this schedule in a non -
CF/DF deal.  
You must also factor in this Minimum Cash when calculating the Cash Flow Available for Debt 
Repayment , as it limits how much Free Cash Flow the company can use to repay Debt principal.  
 
11. How might you estimate this Minimum Cash b alance if the company doesn’t disclose it?  
You might look at how low its Cash balance has fallen historically, or you might look at Cash as a 
% of Total Expenses or Total Cash Expenses and see how that figure has trended in the past.

96 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
For example, if Cash has always been between 5% and 10% of (COGS + OpEx), you might make 
the Minimum Cash balance 7.5% of (COGS + OpEx).  
 
12. How does a  Management Rollover affect the Sources & Uses schedule in an LBO? 
The Management Rollover counts as a Source of Funds because it reduces  the Debt and /or 
Investor Equity required to do the deal.  
For example, if a company’s existing management team rolls over 10 million shares out of 50 
million, the PE firm only needs to purchase 40 million shares . 
The Management Rollover also results in reduced ownership for the PE firm after the deal takes 
place.  
 
13. You’re setting up the Transaction Assumptions for an LBO, but you don’t have any 
information on the Debt Comps. How might you estimate the interest rates on Debt?  
You could also estimate the Interest Rate on Debt by using default spreads , similar to the 
method you sometimes use with Cost of Debt in the WACC calculation.  
Start with the yields of 10 -year government b onds issued by the government of this company’s 
country , and then calculate the company’s interest coverage ratio and leverage ratio to get a 
sense of its credit rating (or use its actual credit rating).  
Then, look up the company’s default spread based on this credit rating and add that to the 10-
year bond yield.  
For example, if the company’s leverage ratio will be 5x after the deal closes , which corresponds 
to a BB+ credit rating, you might look up BB+ -rated companies and find that most of them have 
spreads of 4.0%.  
If the 10 -year government bond yield is 3.0%, the interest rate might  be 3.0% + 4.0% = 7.0%.  
 
14. How do  transaction  and financing fees factor into an LBO model? 
The company or PE firm must pay for these fees upfront in Cash, thereby increasing the 
purchase price, but the accounting treatment of the fees differs.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
97 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Legal & Advisory Fees (e.g., fees paid to investment bankers, lawyers, accountants, etc.) are 
deducted from Cash and Retained Earnings.  
Financing Fees (e.g., fees paid to lenders to arrange for the Debt)  are deducted from the book 
value of the Debt on the L&E side and  Cash on the other side of the Balance Sheet.  
These Financing Fees then amortize over time, increasing the book value of the Debt. If there’s 
a principal repayment, the amortization is accelerated to correspond to the proportion repaid 
(but we often ignore this in models for simplicity).  
Even though the book value of Debt initially declines because of these fees and then rises due 
to the amortization, the company pays interest on the face value  of the Debt. 
For example, for a $100 million Debt issuance  with $3 million in financing fees, the company 
pays interest on the $100 million rather t han the $97 million.

Financial Statement  Projections and the Debt Schedule 
These questions are also more likely to come up in private equity interviews than in investment 
banking interviews.  
However, it is still important to understand Free Cash Flow projections and Debt repayment in 
LBOs, no matter what you’re interviewing for . 
 
1. Can you explain how to adjust the Balance Sheet in an LBO model? 
The adjustments are similar to those in an M&A deal, but in an LBO, you don’t “combine”  the 
Seller’s Balance Sheet with the B uyer’s since the “Buyer” is a holding company.  
You write down the company’s Common Shareholders’ Equity and replace it with the Investor 
Equity the PE firm is contributing, you still create Goodwill and Other Intangible Assets, and you 
might adjust the Deferred Tax -related items as well.  
You also add the new Debt and adjust the existing Debt on the L&E side of the Balance Sheet, 
and you adjust Cash on the Assets side for deal funding and transaction fees. You may also 
write up or down Asset values.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
98 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
You deduct one -time Transaction Fees from Common Shareholders’ Equity  and Financing Fees 
from the book value of the new Debt issued.  
 
2. How is Purchase Price Allocation different in LBO models? Does it matter more or less than 
in M&A deals?  
It’s the same process as  in M&A deals, but it tends to matter far less  because leveraged 
buyouts are based  on cash flow s, Debt repayment, and the IRR from buying and selling a 
company.  
Many of the new items that get created in the PPA process, such as the D&A on Asset Write -
Ups, affect the company’s EPS but barely impact  its cash flow, which is why many LBO models 
skip this schedule.  
 
3. How do you project Free Cash Flow and Cash Flow Available for Debt Repayment in an LBO 
model? 
For Free Cash Flow, you start with Net Income, add back D&A, factor in the Change in Working 
Capital, and subtract CapEx . 
You should not add back Stock- Based Compensation because it creates additional shares, 
reducing the PE firm’s ownership in the company ; it’s easier to treat SBC as a cash operating 
expense.  
You might factor in other items such as Deferred Taxes, but these should not make a huge 
difference for FCF.  
And if the company follows IFRS, you should make sure that the full lease expense is deducted 
in FCF (as it’s split into multiple components).  
Cash Flow Available for Debt Repayment is similar to FCF, but it  adds the company’s Beginning 
Cash and subtracts its Minimum Cash and other obligations , such as mandatory Debt principal 
repayments.  
 
4. How is the “Free Cash Flow” in an LBO model different from the FCF in a DCF?  
First, the purpose  is quite different since FCF in an LBO model determines a company’s ability 
to repay Debt, not the implied value of the entire company.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
99 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Second, FCF in an LBO model starts with Net Income, not NOPAT, and so it deducts the Net 
Interest Expense. But it’s also not Levered FCF  since it does not deduct the Debt principal 
repayments . 
Finally, while FCF is the endpoint in a DCF, you have to go beyond it in an LBO model because of 
the company’s Beginning Cash, Minimum Cash, and other obligations such as required Debt 
principal repayments.  
 
5. Why might a company’s FCF in an LBO model differ from its Cash Flow Available for Debt 
Repayment? 
It might differ because of the additional components that go into Cash Flow Available for Debt 
Repayment: the Beginning Cash, Minimum Cash, and Other Obligations.  
For example, if a company generates $100 in FCF in the first year following an LBO, it won’t 
necessarily be able to repay $100 of Debt  principal.  
If its Beginning Cash is $50 and its Minimum Cash is $75, it will be able to repay only $50 + $100 
– $75 = $75.  
 
6. What does the “tax shield” in an LBO  mean ? 
All it means is that Interest on Debt reduces a company’s taxes because the Interest is tax -
deductible  (within certain limits, depending on the country’s tax laws).  
However, the company’s cash flow is still lower than it would have been WITHOUT the Debt  – 
the tax savings helps, but the additional Interest E xpense still reduces Net Income . 
Some people believe this “tax shield” makes a huge difference in an LBO, but it makes a 
marginal impact next to drivers such as the purchase and exit multiples . 
 
7. How do you set up the formulas for Mandatory and Optional Debt Repayments in an LBO 
model? 
Mandatory Principal Repayment for a tranche of Debt is based  on the percentage that 
amortize s each year, the initial amount of Debt raised, and the amount of Debt  remaining.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
100 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
You should take the minimum between Amortization % * Initial Amount and Debt Remaining 
because you never want to repay  more than the remaining Debt (e.g., 20% * $100 million = $20 
million per year, but if only $10 million is left, repay only $10 million).  
The Optional Debt Repayment formula is similar, but it’s based  on the minimum between the 
Cash Flow Available right now  and the amount of Debt remaining right now . 
For example, if, after Mandatory Repayments, the company has $100 million in cash flow and 
$250 million of Debt remaining, it would repay $100 million.  
But if it had only $50 million remaining, it would repay that entire remaining $50 million.  
 
8. How do cash flow sweeps affect the Optional Repayments?  
The only difference is that with a cash flow sweep, only a percentage  of the Cash Flow Available 
for Debt Repayment is used to repay the Debt principal.  
In the example above, with a 50% sweep, the company would repay $50 million of its Debt 
rather than $100 million because 50% * $100 million = $50 million.  
 
9. How do you use a Revolver in an LBO model?  
You draw on the Revolver when the company doesn’t have enough cash flow to make its 
Mandatory Debt Repayments  while maintaining its Minimum Cash balance.  
For example, let’s say the Beginning Cash is $50, the Mandatory Repayments are $100, the FCF 
is $75, and the Minimum Cash is $100.  
In this case, Cash Flow Available for Debt Repayment = $50 + $75 – $100 – $100 = ($75).  
Therefore, the company must draw on its Revolver for $75 to boost its cash flow to the level 
where it can make the $100 in Mandatory Repayments.  
The company will then pay interest and fees on this additional borrowing, and it will repay the 
Revolver balance as soon as it can do so.  
The Revolver is like a personal overdraft account at a bank.  
 
10. Which metrics and ratios might you calculate in an LBO, and what do they tell you?

101 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Common metrics and ratios include Debt / EBITDA, EBITDA / Interest, and FCF Conversion 
because they give you better insight into how a deal performs over time. They can also indicate 
how risky a deal is  and if the PE firm can use additional Debt to boost its returns.  
For example, if the company goes from 5x Debt / EBITDA to 3x in 1 -2 years, perhaps the PE firm 
could use more Debt initially , or it could do a Dividend Recap to boost its returns.  
And if the company’s FCF Conversion increases from 10% to 30%, the deal is more attractive 
because it’s a sign that more of the returns come from Debt Paydown and Cash Generation.  
 
11. Since an LBO is based  on Free Cash Flow, why do you focus on  EBITDA and TEV / EBITDA in 
the assumptions?  
EBITDA is quick to calculate , and it’s sometimes a reasonable approximation for Cash Flow from 
Operations, so lenders and potential acquirers focus on it.  
But the other reason is that T EV / EBITDA tends to be more stable over time than Equity 
Value -based multiples . 
If you used P  / E or Equity Value / FCF multiples, they might change dramatically as the 
company repays Debt.  
Therefore, it would be more difficult to pick an exit multiple in- line with the purchase multiple.  
Finally, you can use EBITDA and TEV / EBITDA even if the company has negative Net Income 
(due to a high interest  expense, for example).

Exit Strategies  
The most important questions here are the first few on the trade -offs of different exit strategies 
and the relationship between IRR and Money -on-Money (MoM) multiples.  
 
1. What are the different exit strategies available to a private equity  firm in a leveraged 
buyout, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each one?  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
102 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
The main exit strategies are an M&A deal, an initial public offering (IPO), and a dividend 
recapitalization.  
In an M&A Deal, the PE firm sells the company to another company or PE firm. It’s a clean and 
simple break where the firm earns all the deal proceeds in one fell swoop.  
In an IPO Exit, the PE firm takes the company public and sells its shares gradually over time; 
sometimes , companies that can’t be acquired can  go public, which is the main advantage  (e.g., 
the company might be too big to be an acquisition target) . 
But the disadvantage is that the sale of the PE firm’s stake takes much longer, so there’s also 
more risk (e.g., if the company’s share price drops). The firm can’t sell its entire stake all at once 
because it sends a negative signal to other investors.  
In a Dividend Recapitalization, the company issues Divide nds to the PE firm continually or issues 
additional Debt and distributes the proceeds as Dividends, and the PE firm earns  the deal 
proceeds gradually over time.  
It is difficult to earn an acceptable IRR with the Dividend Recap, but sometimes it is the only 
option if the M&A or IPO markets are underdeveloped , or the company has legal or PR issues 
that prevent it from going public or being acquired.  
 
2. What IRR and MoM m ultiple do PE firms typically target ? 
Most private equity firms aim for an IRR of at least 20%, about twice what public equity 
markets  in developed countries  have returned historically.  
The targeted multiple depends on the time frame of each investment, but a 20% IRR over 5 
years equates to a 2.5x multiple, so many firms target multiples in that range.  
If the firm holds companies for longer periods – say, 7 years on average – it may need to target 
a higher multiple, such as 3.5x ( a ~20% IRR over 7 years).  
Most firms also target different numbers for the Base, Upside, and Downside cases and aim to 
avoid losing money  no matter what happens.  
 
3. Would you rather achieve a high IRR or a high MoM multiple in a leveraged buyout ? 
It depends  on the time frame.  Over a short period, such as 6 months , a high IRR, such as 50%, is 
meaningless because you’ve barely made money (~1.25x multiple).  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
103 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
But over a long period, such as 10 years , a high MoM  multiple such as 3 x means less because it 
corresponds to a ~12 % IRR.  
Limited Partners judge private equity funds by their IRRs, but they also don’t want the money 
to be returned to them  too quickly . 
The best answer to this question is: “ PE firms care more about IRR because that’s how they’re 
measured, but over short time frames, it’s better to earn a high multiple, and over longer time 
frames, it’s better to earn a high IRR. Also, if the PE firm has already exceeded its hurdle rate, it 
will focus more on MoM multiple s.” 
 
4. How do you select a proper range of exit multiples in an LBO model?  
You normally look at the purchase multiple, the historical and current market data for the 
company and its peer companies, and how the company’s growth rates, margins, and ROIC 
change over the holding period.  
For example, if the acquired company and peer companies have traded between 10x and 15x 
EBITDA historically, and the company’s ROIC improves significantly in the holding period, you 
might argue for an exit multiple near the top of this range.  
But if its ROIC stagnates or declines, you might pick a multiple near the lower end of the range 
(or below it).  
It’s common to assume the exit multiple equals the purchase multiple, but it’s more defensible 
to assume a modest decline unless the company improves significantly.  
 
5. What might trigger “Multiple Expansion” in an LBO, and is this assumption ever justified?  
A valuation multiple is shorthand  for valuation:  it’s an abbreviated way of expressing a 
company’s Discount Rate, FCF, and FCF Growth Rate.  
So, yes, Multiple Expansion is possible in an LBO . For example, if a company’s Return on 
Invested Capital (ROIC) improves and its WACC stays the same, then its FCF and FCF Growth 
should both increase, which should  boost its exit multiple.  
Some PE firms aim for Multiple Expansion in deals, but it’s tough to predict and depends heavily 
on market conditions .

104 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Even if a PE firm improves a company’s ROIC significantly, the exit multiple might stay the same 
or fall if the overall market has declined.  
 
6. Would you rather have an extra dollar of D ebt paydown  or an extra dollar of EBITDA  in an 
LBO? 
An extra dollar of EBITDA is more beneficial because it increases the company’s Exit Enterprise 
Value by a multiple  of that dollar.  
If the Debt paydown increases by $1, the Exit Equity Proceeds at the end increase by $1 
because the Net Debt is lower by $1.  
But if the Exit Year EBITDA increases by $1, then the Exit Equity Proceeds increase by $1 * [A 
multiple of 5 -10x or more].

Making Investment Decisions 
This section lies in “private equity” territory. Since you do not  make investment decisions as an 
investment banker , these questions are extremely unlikely to come up in IB interviews.  
 
1. Can you walk me through how you might make an investment decision based on an LBO 
model's output?  
You start with the investment  criteria . For example, maybe you’re aiming for a 20% IRR and a 
2.5x multiple in the Base Case and a 1.5x minimum multiple in the Downside Case.  
Then, you build projections and look at the LBO model output in the Base Case.  
If the numbers don’t work, it’s an easy “No”; if they do, you build the Downside Case 
projections and start testing everything there.  
If it seems like you could easily fall below the 1.5x minimum in the Downside Case, you might 
say “No,” but if the worst- case multiple is above that, you might proceed.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
105 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
Finally, you make a decision and back it up with qualitative factors. If the numbers tell you 
“Yes,” you find the qualitative points to support your argument; if they tell you “No,” you find 
the qualitative reasons to reject the deal.  
 
2. Why might you reject a deal even if the IRRs and MoM multiples are favorable in all cases?  
You might reject a deal like this if there’s a problem with the industry, such as a lack of good 
exit strategies or credit markets that won’t support the deal.  
For example, if it seems impossible for the company to go public and there are no likely buyers, 
the PE firm might conclude that it’s impossible to realize  anything close to the returns predicted 
by the LBO model.  
 
3. Why might you RECOMMEND a deal even if the IRRs and MoM multiples are NOT favorable 
across the different cases?  
You might recommend a deal if the numbers are “borderline,” but you believe there’s an easy 
way to boost them above the thresholds.  
For example, if the Base Case IRR is 18%, and the MoM multiple is 1.3x in the Downside Case, 
you might look at the projections, realize the company generates a huge amount of Excess 
Cash, and argue that the company could distribute this Excess Cash or do a  Dividend Recap to 
boost the IRR above 18%.  
You could also argue that a different capital structure that lets the company repay more Debt 
principal would result in a higher IRR.  
 
4. What makes an industry more appealing or less appealing to invest in ? 
An industry  is more appealing if it’s growing at a solid pace and highly fragmented and the 
target company is in one of the top 2 – 3 positions.  
These qualities make the industry appealing because the PE firm can acquire other companies 
and make the original company bigger, resulting in a higher market share and, hopefully, a 
higher valuation.  
Strong barriers to entry also help, but they are less likely in fragmented markets.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
106 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
An industry  is less appealing if it’s highly consolidated (e.g., 2 – 3 companies own 80% of the 
market), in decline (newspapers), or highly speculative (asteroid mining).  
A high rate of technological change and low barriers to entry also make an industry less 
appealing because stable cash flows are less likely.  
 
5. If a company has $10 million in revenue and $5 million in EBITDA, is it best to grow by 
selling 20% more units, raising prices by 20%, or cutting expenses by 20%?  
It’s most appealing if it grows by raising prices by 20%.  
If the company does this, everything will “flow through” to EBITDA: The $2 million in extra 
revenue will result in an additional $2 million of EBITDA.  
If the company sells 20% more units, it will incur higher variable costs, and its EBITDA will 
increase by less than $2 million.  
An expense reduction of 20% will result in only $1 million of additional EBITDA, which also 
makes less of an impact than the price increase.  
Investors tend to favor companies with significant pricing power  because it means they have 
less competition and can grow with less friction.  
 
6. How might a PE firm reduce its downside risk if a leveraged buyout does not perform well?  
Much of the risk in leveraged buyouts comes from multiple contraction : The Exit Multiple might 
be lower than the Purchase Multiple.  
One way to reduce this risk is to avoid acquiring companies trading at high multiples (vs. peer 
companies and historical multiples) and to focus on companies that are undervalued  in some 
way.  
Acquiring companies with significant Tangible Assets or non -core divisions that could be sold off 
also reduces the risk  because the PE firm could recover some of its capital by selling these.  
A PE firm could also use structured investments , such as Preferred or Convertible Preferred 
Stock, because these effectively “lock in” a certain rate of return, limiting the losses in 
downside cases.  
Some Common Equity is always required, but even an 80% Common/ 20% Preferred split helps 
in downside cases.

7. How would you review a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) or other marketing 
materials and decide whether to do further research on a potential buyout candidate?  
You might start by reading the first few pages of the Executive Summary in the beginning to 
assess the company’s industry, size, and possible valuation.  
Then, you would skip to the historical and projected financial statements toward the end to see 
if the LBO math works. If earning a 20% IRR seems impossible, even with optimistic projections, 
you might reject the company immediately.  
But if the math seems plausible, you might keep reading and go to the market/industry 
overview section to assess the industry growth rates, the competitors, and this company’s 
appeal vs. peers in the industry.  
If all that checks out, you might read through the entire document, including the management 
team, the customers and suppliers, and the products and services.  
 
8. After reading a company’s CIM, you decide to meet with the CEO. What are the top 3 
questions you would ask?  
You’d focus on questions not  answered  in the CIM, so the best inquiries depend heavily on the 
company, its industry, and how much information has already been disclosed.  
For example, if the CIM provides financial projections but little detail on the revenue and 
expense assumptions, and it seems like the deal might depend on add -on acquisitions, you 
might ask the following questions:  
1) “What’s driving these assumptions for revenue growth of XX% and operating margins of 
YY%?”  
 
2) “What’s your company’s big -picture strategy, and what do you see as the best sources 
of growth?”  
 
3) “Can you tell us about your competitors and smaller companies in the market that might 
be open to acquisitions?”

More Advanced LBO Features  
These topics are unlikely  to come up in interviews, but we still wanted to provide example 
questions and answers on the more advanced topics.  
 
1. When might a PE firm use a Dividend Recap in a leveraged buyout?  
A PE firm might do this if the company pays off significant Debt midway through the holding 
period or can support more Debt at that point (e.g., its EBITDA increases significantly  so that it 
can support another 1 – 2x of Debt).  
If a deal performs well, a Dividend Recap boosts the IRR by allowing the PE firm to earn 
proceeds from the deal earlier. The MoM multiple doesn’t necessarily change, but the IRR 
increases due to the time value of money.  
 
2. Walk me through how the Balance Sheet  and IRR in an LBO change with a $100 Dividend 
Recap and $2 in financing fees. 
On t he Assets side of the Balance Sheet, you deduct the $2 in financing fees from Cash, so the 
Assets side is down by $2.  
 Advanced Financial Modeling Course  
 
109 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
On the L&E side, you record $100 – $2 = $98 for the new Debt because you deduct financing 
fees directly from the book value of the Debt.  
You also deduct $100 from Common Shareholders’ Equity to reflect the Dividends issued to the 
PE firm, so the L&E side is down by $2, and both sides balance.  
In the IRR calculation, you reflect this $100 in Dividends to the PE firm, which boosts the IRR, 
assuming the overall deal performs well.  
 
3. The Investor Equity in an LBO is $500. The Exit Equity Proceeds in Year 5 are normally 
$1,200, but the PE firm decides to execute a $400 Dividend Recap in Year 3. How can you 
quickly estimate the impact on the equity multiple and the IRR?  
Without the Dividend Recap, it’s a 2.4x multiple over 5 years, which is between a 15% and 25% 
IRR (2x = ~15% and 3x = ~25%). We’d say “Just under 20%” as an estimate (it’s 19% in Excel).  
With the Dividend Recap, $400 of the Exit Equity Proceeds shift to a Dividend in Year 3, and the 
Exit Equity Proceeds in Year 5 fall by $400.  
If you assign a 1/3 “weighting” to the Dividend Recap proceeds ($400 / $1200) and 2/3 
weighting to the now -lower Exit Proceeds ($800 / $1200), the “average exit year” is closer to 4 
rather than 5.  
Since a 2x multiple in 3 years is a ~25% IRR and a 3x multiple in 5 years is also a ~25% IRR, it’s 
reasonable to assume that a 2.4x multiple in 4 years is “Just below a ~25% IRR” because 4 years 
is in between 3 and 5, and the 2.4x is in between 2.0x and 3. 0x. In Excel, it’s a 23% IRR.  
 
4. How would you model a “waterfall returns” structure where different E quity investors in 
an LBO receive differ ent percentages of the returns based on the overall IRR?  
For example, let’s say that Investor Group A receives 10% of the returns up to a 15% IRR 
(Investor Group B receives 90%), but then receives 15% of the returns (with Investor Group B 
receiving 85%) above a 15% IRR. How does that work?  
The exact Excel formulas get tricky, but here is the basic idea: 
• First, you check  the IRR for the Exit Equity P roceeds generated in the deal . For example, 
let’s say the deal generates $500 million in Exit Equity Proceeds; you do the calculations 
and find that $500 million equates to an 18% IRR for this period.

110 of 112 https://breakingintowallstreet.com  
• Next, you determine the Exit Equity P roceeds that represent a 15% IRR . Here, you run 
the numbers and find that $450 million equates to a 15% IRR.  
 
• You al locate 10% of this $450 million, or $45 million, to Investor Group A, and 90%, or 
$405 million, to Investor Group B.  
 
• Then, you allocate 15% of the remaining $50 million ($500 million minus $450 million) 
to Investor Group A and 85% to Investor Group B.  
 
5. Why might a private equity firm create a management options pool in an LBO, and how 
does it affect the model? 
The PE firm does this to incentivize management to perform well in exchange for no upfront 
costs or dilution.  
If a deal performs well and the Exit Equity Value exceeds the initial Investor Equity, a small 
percentage of the Exit Equity Proceeds go es to management, barely reducing the IRR for the PE 
firm while greatly increasing the financial results for the management team.  
If the deal does not perform well, and the Exit Equity Value is below the initial Investor Equity, 
nothing is paid to the management team, and the PE firm loses nothing.  
 
6. Walk me through the impact of a 10% options pool in an LBO if the initial Investor Equity is 
$500 and the Exit Equity Value is $1,000.  
The options are in the money  because the Exit Equity Value exceeds the initial Investor Equity . 
You can use the “quick and dirty” method for options to say that the Cash Payment  to the 
company to exercise these options is 10% * $500 = $50, and the Proceeds to Management  are 
10% * $1,000 = $100.  
So, the PE firm now gets $1,000 + $50 – $100 = $950 in exit proceeds instead of $1,000.  
This will modestly reduce the firm’s IRR and multiple, but if this incentive structure resulted in a 
higher Exit Equity Value, it may have been worth the trade -off. 
NOTE:  The “quick and dirty” method here is not quite correct because exercising these options 
expands the total equity pool in the exit. The numbers are the same with the “correct” method, 
but it more accurately reflects reality ( see the coverage in this guide ).

7. How do add -on acquisitions affect the IRR and financial statements in an LBO?  
With add -on acquisitions, you assume that the PE firm uses additional Debt (and, sometimes, 
Equity) to acquire other companies and combine them with the original company.  
You’ll add the additional Debt (and Equity) to the Balance Sheet and add the acquired 
companies’ revenue, expense, and cash flow contributions to the statements.  
The IRR could increase or decrease depending on the numbers; higher -yielding add -on 
acquisitions (e.g., the EBITDA / Purchase Enterprise Value exceeds the original company’s) tend 
to increase the IRR, while lower- yielding ones tend to reduce it.  
But it also depends on the funding method: It’s easier to make add -on acquisitions work with 
100% Debt funding because the PE firm doesn’t contribute any Equity, which boosts its IRR.  
 
8. How does a stub period affect all the calculations in an LBO model?  
A “stub period” means that the deal closes not at the end of the company’s fiscal year but in 
between fiscal years (e.g., at the end of a quarter or a month).  
If there’s a stub period, you must  “roll forward” the company’s last Balance Sheet to the 
transaction close date and make all the adjustments based on that  Balance Sheet instead.  
You also have to project the company’s financial statements, or at least its cash flow, Debt 
repayment, and Cash generation, for the months in this stub period, and use the Balance Sheet 
figures from the end of the stub period for the first full year in the model.  
You also must  use XIRR rather than IRR to calculate the deal’s IRR because of this irregular 
period in the beginning.  
 
9. Walk me through the impact of a $1,000 Shareholder Loan with 10% PIK Interest and 
explain why PE firms use Shareholder Loans in leveraged buyouts . 
A Shareholder Loan (SHL) lets a PE firm label its Investor Equity “Debt” and use it to reduce the 
company’s taxes . 10% PIK Interest lets a PE firm “deduct” a 10% IRR per year for tax purposes . 
On the Income Statement, you record 10% * $1,000 = $100 in PIK Interest and add back that 
$100 on the CFS since it is non -cash. This $100 in PIK Interest accrues to the SHL principal.

The SHL keeps increasing each year, as does the PIK Interest shown on the Income Statement.

Upon exit, this “Shareholder Loan” still counts as Equity, so the PE firm must repay all the real 
Debt first, and it still earns the Exit Equity Proceeds. The only difference is that you may show 
the Shareholder Loan separately from the rest of these Exit Equity Proceeds.  
 
10. Why would a company want to use Pref erred Stock in an LBO if it's the most expensive 
funding source outside of Common Equity?  
It’s not that a company would “want” to use Preferred Stock – it might have to  use it if it has 
already used significant Debt across all its other tranches and the existing lenders are not 
willing to fund anything additional (e.g., Debt / EBITDA is already 6x, and the company wants to 
borrow even more).  
Also, in some cases, the PE investors might use Preferred Stock to invest because it reduces 
their downside risk  by locking in a guaranteed return via the Preferred Coupon Rate. In this 
case, the Preferred Dividends are almost always PIK to avoid a huge cash burden.  
 
11. How do Subordinated Notes with call premiums affect a PE firm's exit strategy in a 
leveraged buyout?  
Call premiums make it more expensive to repay Debt principal early – for example, the 
company might have to repay 105% or 103% of the principal rather than 100%.  
These premiums are higher in the early years and decline over time.  
As a result, they incentivize a PE firm to hold onto a company for a longer period rather than 
selling it for a “quick flip” – as doing so would  result in higher penalty fees and less in Exit Equity 
Proceeds for the PE firm.
