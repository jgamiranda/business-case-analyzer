Finance Concepts  
 
Time Value of Money: Money  today is worth MORE  than money 
tomorrow because you could invest it today and earn more by 
tomorrow.  
 
 
 
Discount Rate:  Measures both the risk  and the potential returns  of an 
investment; represents the annualized returns you ’re expect ing or 
targeting on other , similar investments. 
 
Net Present Value  (NPV): Present Value – Upfront Cost or Asking Price . 
 IRR (Internal Rate of Return):  The annualized return you expect to earn 
from this specific investment , regardless of what similar ones offer.  Like 
the compound interest rate on a simple investment.  
 
Invest When:  IRR > Discount Rate or NPV > 0 . 
Do Not Invest When:  IRR < Discount Rate or NPV < 0 . 
 
Balance Sheet  
 
Provides a snapshot of a company’s 
Assets (its resources) and Liabilities 
and Equity  (its funding sources) at a 
specific point in time; Assets must 
always equal Liabilities + Equity.  
 
Assets:  Can be sold for cash or 
provide a future economic benefit. 
Cash, Investments, Accounts 
Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid 
Expenses, Net PP&E, Goodwill, Other 
Intangibles, Lease Assets, and 
Deferred Tax Assets.  
 
Liabilities:  Funding sources  and 
future obligations / cash outflows. 
Accounts Payable, Accrued Liabilities, 
Deferred Revenue, Lease Liabilities, 
Pensions  Liabilities , and Debt.  
 
Equity:  Additional funding sources 
related to external parties (stock issuances) or internally generated 
via after -tax profits . 
 
Accounting Interview Questions  
 
Record Revenue  and Expenses  only when the product /service is 
delivered  or allocated . If there’s no delivery/allocation , use BS  line 
items like Deferred Revenue and Prepaid Expenses for tracking.  
 
Ending AR  = Beginning AR + Non -Cash Rev. – Cash Collections of Rev.  Income Statement  
 
Shows a company’s revenue, 
expenses, and taxes over a 
PERIOD, such as 1 year, 1 
quarter, or 1 month; items must 
correspond to delivery or 
allocation in the period  and 
affect the income available to 
the common shareholders.  
 
Common Lines:  Revenue, Cost 
of Goods Sold (COGS), Operating 
Expenses, Depreciation &

Interest Income/Expense , and 
Taxes.  
 
Summary Lines:  Gross Profit  
(Revenue – COGS), Operating

Cash Flow Statement  
 
Shows  a company’s cash inflows and 
outflows over a period; items may be 
adjustments to IS line items or additional 
cash inflows/outflows not on the IS.  
 
Cash Flow from Operations:  Net Income, 
Non- Cash Adjustments, and changes to 
Current Assets and Liabilities, excluding Debt 
and Cash.  
 
Cash Flow from Investing:  Changes to many 
Long -Term Assets and some non -operational 
Current Assets.  
 
Cash Flow from Financing:  Changes to many 
Long -Term Liabilities and Equity lines ; also, 
some non -operational Current Liabilities  
(e.g., Short -Term Debt).  
 
Asset UP / DOWN   Cash Flow DOWN  / UP  
Liability UP / DOWN   Cash Flow UP / DOWN   
 
Accounting Interview Questions  
 
1) START with the Income Statement and explain how/if it changes.  
 
2) Then, explain how the Cash Flow Statement changes, if at all 
(including changes in Deferred Taxes).  
 
3) Finish by explaining how the Balance Sheet changes  and why it still 
balances, i.e., why Assets still equal Liabilities + Equity.  Make sure you 
explain changes in Cash and Equity.

Equity Value & Enterprise Value  
 
Equity Value  is the value of 
EVERYTHING  a company 
has (Net Assets, or Total 
Assets – Total Liabilities), 
but only to the EQUITY 
INVESTORS  (the common 
shareholders).  
 
Enterprise Value  is the 
value of the company’s 
CORE BUSINESS 
OPERATIONS  (Net 
Operating Assets, or 
Operating Assets – 
Operating Liabilities), but 
to ALL INVESTORS  (Equity, Debt, Preferred, and possibly others).  
 
Enterprise Value  = Equity Value – Cash + Debt + Preferred Stock + 
Noncontrolling Interests . 
(More Advanced:  Subtract Investments, Non- Core Assets, and NOLs; 
add Unfunded Pensions, other funding sources, and maybe Leases .) 
 
Diluted Equity Value  = Share Price * ( Basic Shares + Dilutive Shares 
from Options, Warrants, RSUs, Convertibles, etc. ) 
 
Treasury Stock Method:  If the Options are in the money, Diluted 
Shares = # Options – # Options * Exercise Price / Current Share Price.  
Add these to the Basic Shares when calculating the Diluted Shares.  
 
Does Common Shareholders’ Equity (CSE) Change?  If so, then Equity 
Value change s by that amount. If not, it does not change.  
 
Do Net Operating Assets (NOA) Change?  If so, then Enterprise Value 
(TEV)  changes by that amount. If not, it does not change.  
 
EX: A company issues $100 of common stock to buy a  factory. CSE is up 
by $100, so Equity Value is up by $100. The factory is in Net PP&E, a

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis  
 
A DCF values a compan y 
based on the Present Value of its future cash 
flows  over the next 5 – 
10 years plus  their 
Present Value after that, in the “Terminal Period”  
(whose value you estimate with the 
Terminal Value).  
 
You almost always use an Unlevered DCF , which uses Unlevered Free 
Cash Flow and the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) for the 
Discount Rate.  Terminal Value  could be based on the Multiples 
Method (multiply the final 
forecast year EBITDA by a 
multiple from the comps) 
or the Gordon Growth 
Method  (see right).  
 
Unlevered FCF  = Operating Income * (1 – Tax Rate) + D&A +/ - Change 
in Working Capital – CapEx  
(More Advanced:  There may be adjustments for Deferred Taxes, 
Acquisitions, Intangible Purchases, Capitalized R&D, and Non-Recurring 
Charges. Always deduct  the full Lease Expense for simplicity.)  
 
ALWAYS EXCLUDE  Interest Expense/Income, Preferred Dividends, Debt 
Principal Repayments, and Stock Issuances/Repurchases. 
 
Like EBITDA, UFCF is not  affected by the company’s capital structure 
(Debt, Equity, Interest, etc.). However, the Discount Rate (WACC) is  
affected by capital structure, so the DCF output could still change.  
 
Implied Enterprise Value in a DCF  = Present Value of UFCF in Explicit 
Forecast Period + Present Value of Terminal Value   Then, add Cash, 
subtract Debt, etc., to get the Implied Equity Value.

 Discount Rate  (WACC)  
 The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)  in a DCF represents the 
average annualized return you might expect to earn by investing in the entire capital structure (Equity, Debt, Preferred, etc.) of similar 
companies in the market. To a company, it represents the cost of 
funding its operations via all its sources of capital.

In Cost of Equity, you normally “un- lever” Beta for each comparable 
company, take the median, and then re -lever it based on the subject 
company’s capital structure:  
 
• Unlevered Beta  = Levered Beta / (1 + Debt / Equity * (1 – Tax 
Rate) + Preferred Stock / Equity)  
• (Re-)Levered Beta  = Unlevered Beta * (1 + Debt / Equity * (1 – 
Tax Rate) + Preferred Stock / Equity)  
 Equity is more expensive than Debt because its risks and potential 
returns are both higher; also, the interest paid on Debt is tax -deductible, 
which makes the cash costs cheaper for the company.  
 
M&A and Merger Models  
 
Merger Model Walkthrough:  1) Project Buyer/Seller s tatements.  
 2) Estimate the Purchase Price and Cash, Debt, and Stock funding.  
 
3) Create Sources & Uses and Purchase Price Allocation schedules.  
 4) Combine the Balance Sheets and reflect Cash, Debt, and Stock used, 
new Goodwill, and Seller’s Common Equity write -down. 
 5) Combine Income Statements, including Foregone Interest on Cash, 
Interest on New Debt, and Synergies.  
 6) Combined Net Income = Combined Pre- Tax Income * (1 – Buyer’s Tax 
Rate); Combined EPS = Combined Net Income / (Buyer’s Shares + Shares 
Issued in Deal)  
 7) EPS Accretion / Dilution = Combined EPS / Buyer’s Standalone EPS – 1

M&A and Merger Models  
 
Deals are accretive  when the Combined EPS is higher than the Buyer’s 
standalone EPS and dilutive  when it’s lower : 
 
Component:  Calculation  
Cost of Cash:  Foregone Interest Rate on Cash * (1 – Buyer’s 
Tax Rate)  
Cost of Debt:  Interest Rate on New Debt * (1 – Buyer’s Tax 
Rate)  
Cost of Stock:  Buyer’s  Net Income / Buyer’s  Equity Value  
Weighted Cost of 
Acquisition:  % Cash Used * Cost of Cash + % Debt Used * 
Cost of Debt + % Stock Used * Cost of Stock  
Seller’s Purchase Yield:  Seller’s Net Income / Purchase Equity Value  
Accretive/Dilutive?  Accretive if Weighted Cost < Purchase Yield; 
Dilutive if Weighted Cost > Purchase Yield  
 
If the deal is 100% Stock, compare the Buyer’s P / E multiple to the 
Seller’s Purchase P / E multiple. If the Buyer’s multiple is higher, the 
deal will be accretive; if not, it will be dilutive.  
 
M&A Purchase Price:  Based on a premium to the Seller’s current share 
price for public companies; for private companies, use a multiple such 
as TEV / EBITDA.  The “true price” is the Purchase Enterprise Value.  
 
Financing Mix:  Buyers use as much Cash as possible first, then Debt, 
and then Stock because of their respective costs. Cash is limited based 
on Min. Cash requirements, and Debt is normally limited based on a 
max or targeted Debt / EBITDA.  
 
Sources & Uses:  Demonstrates where the funding comes from and 
where it goes to; existing Debt is almost always refinanced and 
replaced with a new issuance of the same size (terms may differ).

 LBO Model Walkthrough:  1) Assume a Purchase Price, Debt and Equity 
used, Interest Rate on Debt, and Operational Drivers (revenue growth, margins, etc.).  
 2) Create a Sources & Uses schedule to reflect the Debt and Investor 
Equity used, Fees, and treatment of the company’s Cash and Debt.  
 
3) Project the company’s Income Statement and a partial Cash Flow 
Statement down to the Free Cash Flow (FCF) line.  
 4) CF Avail. For Debt Repayment = Beginning Cash + FCF – Minimum 
Cash (and subtract Mandatory Debt Repayments if applicable).  
 
5) Repay this amount of Debt each year (and the mandatory 
repayments) and link the Interest Expense to this changing Debt balance, ensuring that FCF also deducts the Interest.  
 6) Assume an EBITDA exit multiple, back into the Exit Equity Value, and 
calculate the IRR and MoM multiple based on these Exit Equity Proceeds 
vs. the Investor Equity in the beginning.  
 Ideal LBO Candidates:  Stable  / predictable cash flows, modest CapEx, 
fragmented industry, growth  / margin opportunities, strong 
management, solid asset bases, and realistic paths to exit.  
 Debt Types:  Revolver (temporary borrowing), Secured Debt (Lower 
floating rates, principal repayments, collateral, maintenance covenants), 
and Unsecured Debt (Higher fixed rates, no principal repayments, and 
incurrence covenants ). Draw on Revolver when CF Avail. For Debt 
Repayment turns negative and repay when it’s positive.  
 
Returns Sources:  Multiple Expansion (buy for 10x EBITDA and sell for 
12x), EBITDA Growth, and Debt Paydown / Cash Generation.  
 
Returns Drivers:  Purchase and Exit Assumptions matter most; % Debt 
used is very significant; revenue growth, margin, and cash flow 
assumptions are also important. Specific Debt terms matter less.  
 
LBO Valuation:  Use Goal Seek to solve for the maximum price  a PE firm 
could pay to achieve its targeted IRR, such as 20%, with the operational, exit, and debt  / equity assumptions as “constants.”  
 LBO Math  (for “in between” values, approximate  via averages ): 
 
Multiple and Time Frame:  Approximate IRR:  
2x in 3 Years  ~25%  
2x in 5 Years  ~15%  
3x in 3 Years  ~45%  
3x in 5 Years  ~25%  
 
EX: Buy a $100 EBITDA company for 10x EBITDA and 6x Debt / EBITDA, 
sell at $150 EBITDA for 9x  in 5 years , and repay  $250 of Debt. Exit TEV = 
$150 *9 = $1,350. Subtract the $600 – $250 = $350 of remaining Debt. 
$1,000 Exit Equity Proceeds / $400 Investor Equity = 2.5x, so it’s a ~20% 
IRR over 5 years since 2.5x is right between 2x (~15%) and 3x (~25%).
